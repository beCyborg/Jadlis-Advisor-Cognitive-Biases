# Tier 2 Biases: Decision Framing and Choice

This reference covers six Tier 2 biases that distort how options are perceived, valued, and chosen. All six operate at the interface between how a situation is represented and how the decision-maker responds to that representation. They are interconnected: framing determines the reference point, loss aversion drives the endowment effect and sunk cost fallacy, and commitment bias locks in prior choices even as evidence deteriorates.

---

## Framing Effect

**Tier 2 | Effect size: d = 0.31–0.60 | Replication: Robust across domains**

### Mechanism

The framing effect occurs because humans evaluate options relative to a reference point, not in absolute terms. The same objective information triggers systematically different responses depending on whether it is presented as a gain or a loss, a positive attribute or a negative one, a probability of survival or a probability of death.

The mechanism is rooted in Kahneman and Tversky's Prospect Theory (1979): the value function is S-shaped, concave in the gain domain (risk aversion) and convex in the loss domain (risk seeking). When the same choice is framed as avoiding a loss, the loss-domain portion of the curve activates, producing stronger aversion than the equivalent gain framing would produce attraction. This asymmetry means that logically equivalent descriptions produce reliably different preference orderings.

Three subtypes operate through partially distinct pathways:
- **Risky-choice framing**: choice between a certain outcome and a gamble (classic Asian Disease problem). Effect size d ≈ 0.50–0.60.
- **Attribute framing**: a single characteristic described positively or negatively ("80% fat-free" vs. "20% fat"). Effect size d ≈ 0.31–0.45.
- **Goal framing**: emphasis on positive vs. negative consequences of taking an action. Effect size d ≈ 0.35.

### Evidence Base

Tversky & Kahneman (1981) demonstrated the classic risky-choice framing reversal with the Asian Disease problem: when saving 200 lives was framed as a gain, 72% chose the certain option; when the same outcome was framed as 400 deaths, 78% chose the gamble. Logically identical; behaviorally opposite.

Meta-analyses (Kühberger, 1998; Levin et al., 1998) confirm the effect across hundreds of studies. Effect sizes are largest in medical and health contexts (d ≈ 0.60) and smallest in purely financial contexts with experienced decision-makers (d ≈ 0.31). Deliberative processing and domain expertise reduce but do not eliminate the effect.

### Domain Manifestations

**Business.** Product marketing exploits attribute framing routinely: "95% bacteria killed" outperforms "5% bacteria survive." Medical device approvals framed as mortality reduction versus survival rates produce different physician adoption patterns. The same product feature can shift market reception substantially based on frame selection.

**Finance.** Investment communications framed around potential gains attract different investor profiles than those framed around downside protection, even when expected values are identical. Portfolio managers who see quarterly returns framed as losses relative to a benchmark trade more aggressively than those seeing equivalent underperformance framed as near-achievement.

**Personal.** Medical treatment adherence is strongly frame-dependent. Presenting surgical risk as "5% mortality" rather than "95% survival" reduces willingness to proceed even among patients who understand the equivalence intellectually. Plea bargains are accepted less frequently when defendants frame their baseline state as freedom (any sentence = loss) rather than as the potential sentence (plea = large gain relative to trial verdict).

### Debiasing Procedure

1. Identify the current frame: is the option presented as a gain, a loss, or neutral?
2. Explicitly restate the same option in the opposite frame. Write both versions down.
3. Identify the reference point being used. Ask: whose reference point is this, and is it the correct one for this decision?
4. Restate the options in terms of final states rather than changes. Instead of "+200 lives saved" or "-400 lives lost," use "outcome A: 200 survive, 400 die; outcome B: 1/3 chance all 600 survive, 2/3 chance all 600 die."
5. If deliberation time allows, require explicit written justification for the choice. Externally prompted reasoning activates System 2 processing and reduces frame sensitivity by approximately 30–40% in controlled conditions.

### Example

A pharmaceutical company presents a new drug to physicians. Framing A: "This treatment has a 90% survival rate at five years." Framing B: "10% of patients on this treatment die within five years." Physicians presented with Framing A recommend the drug significantly more often than those presented with Framing B, despite identical information. Debiasing intervention: present both frames simultaneously in writing before eliciting recommendation.

## Sunk Cost Fallacy

**Tier 2 | Effect size: d = 0.43 | Replication: Robust, cross-cultural**

### Mechanism

The sunk cost fallacy is the tendency to continue a course of action because of previously invested resources (time, money, effort) that cannot be recovered, even when discontinuation is the objectively superior option based on prospective costs and benefits alone.

The proximate cause is loss aversion: abandoning a project crystallizes the loss, making it psychologically real. Continuing the project keeps the loss in a probabilistic future state where recovery remains technically possible. This asymmetry in loss realization drives continued investment beyond the rational threshold.

Secondary mechanisms amplify the effect:
- **Framing of abandonment as failure**: quitting is cognitively categorized as a loss event, triggering loss aversion on top of the sunk cost calculation.
- **Regret anticipation**: decision-makers model future regret from quitting more vividly than regret from continued failure.
- **Consistency motivation**: prior investment signals prior commitment; abandonment creates self-inconsistency (see Commitment Bias).

### Evidence Base

Arkes & Blumer (1985) conducted the foundational experimental series. In one study, participants who paid more for a ski trip chose to attend it over a more enjoyable cheaper trip at significantly higher rates (54% vs. lower rates for the cheaper trip condition). The irrationality is clean: the payment is non-refundable in both conditions, so it should be decision-irrelevant.

Effect size d = 0.43 (meta-analysis, Mathieu & Zajac, 1990; updated estimates in Sleesman et al., 2012). The effect is stronger when the prior decision was made publicly (visibility increases commitment pressure), when the decision-maker personally made the prior investment, and when the escalation decision is also made by the same person. Effect is reduced with age (older adults show significantly less susceptibility), and reduced by accountability to external evaluators who were not involved in the original decision.

The Concorde aircraft program is the canonical real-world case: the British and French governments continued funding development after it became clear the project would never be commercially viable, explicitly because of prior commitments — generating the term "Concorde fallacy."

### Domain Manifestations

**Business.** Product development teams continue building features for products that have failed market validation because of engineering hours already spent. Companies persist with failing marketing campaigns past rational stopping points because of agency fees already paid. Projects exceed budget not through single large misjudgments but through serial small escalation decisions driven by sunk costs.

**Finance.** Investors hold losing positions beyond rational stop-loss points, waiting for recovery to "break even." The break-even reference point is a sunk cost artifact — the purchase price has no bearing on what the asset will do prospectively. This produces asymmetric portfolio behavior: winners are sold too quickly (gains are realized), losers are held too long (losses are deferred).

**Personal.** Continuing in careers, educational programs, or relationships because of time already invested. Finishing meals, films, or books that are not worth the time, because the entry cost was paid. The exit decision should be based solely on the marginal value of continued engagement.

### Debiasing Procedure

1. Explicitly label the prior investment as a sunk cost. State: "This [amount/time] is gone regardless of what I decide now."
2. Reframe the decision as if starting fresh today. Ask: "If I had not yet made any investment in this, would I choose to begin it now given current information?"
3. Enumerate only forward-looking costs and benefits: what will it cost to continue? What is the expected value of continuation? What is the expected value of stopping?
4. Assign the decision to someone who was not involved in the original commitment. Their analysis will be sunk-cost-free.
5. Set explicit pre-commitment criteria: define in advance the conditions under which you will exit, before investment begins.

### Example

A startup has spent $800,000 building a SaaS product. Customer interviews reveal that the target market does not have the problem the product solves. The rational decision is to pivot or shut down. Instead, the founding team continues for 18 months, spending an additional $600,000, reasoning that they cannot abandon what they have built. Debiasing: present the decision to an advisor who joins post-investment. Their recommendation (pivot or shut down) is based on prospective value only.

## Commitment Bias / Escalation of Commitment

**Tier 2 | Effect size: d = 0.37 | Replication: Robust in organizational contexts**

### Mechanism

Commitment bias is the tendency to maintain prior behavioral choices, particularly those made publicly, even when evidence shows they are not producing desired outcomes. It is related to but distinct from the sunk cost fallacy: sunk cost fallacy is driven by past investment, commitment bias is driven by the need for self-consistency and face-saving.

Barry Staw (1976) first formalized the phenomenon. The core mechanism involves cognitive dissonance: abandoning a course of action you have publicly advocated creates inconsistency between self-image ("I am a competent decision-maker") and behavior ("I made a wrong call"). To resolve this dissonance, individuals either (a) continue the failed course or (b) selectively process information to reframe the situation as not-failing.

Commitment bias intensifies when:
- The original commitment was public (reputational stakes are activated)
- The decision-maker has personal responsibility for the initial choice
- Abandonment is culturally coded as weakness rather than wisdom
- Leadership role creates pressure to appear resolute

The bias has a useful variant: commitment to long-horizon goals where early failure is expected and persistence is genuinely adaptive. The debiasing challenge is distinguishing legitimate persistence from irrational commitment maintenance.

### Evidence Base

Staw (1976): participants who made a prior financial allocation decision and received negative feedback subsequently allocated more resources to the failing course than participants who inherited the negative situation from someone else. Personal responsibility for the original decision was the critical driver.

Meta-analysis (Sleesman et al., 2012): d = 0.37 across 166 studies, N > 10,000. Moderators: public commitment increases effect by ~0.15d; external accountability to uninvolved parties decreases effect by ~0.12d. Organizational studies find that CEOs escalate commitment to failing product development programs significantly more when they were the original project champion.

The DARE drug prevention program in the United States continued receiving federal funding for decades despite multiple randomized controlled trial evaluations showing no effect on drug use rates — and some evidence of iatrogenic effects — because of political and institutional commitment to the program's continued existence.

### Domain Manifestations

**Business.** Executives continue strategic initiatives long past rational exit points because changing course is perceived as admitting error. Board members resist acknowledging that a CEO hire was wrong, continuing to support ineffective leadership. Organizations persist with technology platforms past their utility horizon because migration requires admitting the original platform selection was suboptimal.

**Finance.** Fund managers maintain positions in declining assets beyond analytical justification because their thesis is on record with investors. Changing the position requires acknowledging the prior thesis was wrong, which is reputationally costly.

**Personal.** Remaining in careers chosen at 18 because changing direction "wastes" the prior investment. Maintaining political affiliations past the point of genuine alignment because switching parties feels like admitting prior error. Following diets or fitness programs that are demonstrably not working because stopping signals failure.

### Debiasing Procedure

1. Explicitly separate the evaluation of the current situation from the evaluation of the original decision. The original decision may have been correct given information at the time; its correctness is irrelevant to the current choice.
2. Conduct a prospective analysis: "Knowing only what I know today about current conditions, would I choose this course of action if I were deciding for the first time?"
3. Assign the exit decision to a person or group with no stake in the original commitment. Fresh evaluators are not subject to the face-saving pressure that drives escalation.
4. Create pre-commitment exit criteria at decision initiation. Document: "We will discontinue this program if X does not occur by date Y." Then treat the criteria as binding.
5. Reframe course correction as evidence of learning and decision quality, not as evidence of original error. Leaders who update based on evidence demonstrate adaptive competence.

### Example

A company's CTO selected a microservices architecture three years ago. Current engineering evidence shows the architecture is creating more complexity than it resolves for the team's size and product maturity. The CTO continues defending the architecture publicly and allocates resources to extend it, because acknowledging the mismatch would require acknowledging the original selection judgment was wrong. Debiasing: commission a technical audit by engineers who joined after the architecture decision, with explicit mandate to evaluate fit-for-purpose prospectively.

## Endowment Effect

**Tier 2 | Effect size: g = 0.57 | Replication: Replicated with some boundary conditions**

### Mechanism

The endowment effect is the tendency to value an object more highly simply because you own it, compared to the valuation you would assign the identical object if you did not own it. This produces a systematic gap between willingness-to-accept (WTA, seller price) and willingness-to-pay (WTA, buyer price) for the same good.

The primary mechanism is loss aversion: when you own an object, selling it is coded as a loss (you lose the object), whereas buying it would have been coded as a gain. Because losses loom larger than equivalent gains (ratio approximately 1.5–2.5x in most studies), the owner's minimum acceptable price exceeds the non-owner's maximum willingness to pay.

Secondary mechanisms:
- **Reference price theory**: owners anchor on the acquisition price as the fair price, even when market conditions have changed.
- **Mere ownership effect**: mere possession triggers positive associations with the owned object, increasing its subjective value independently of loss aversion.
- **Psychological ownership**: even temporary or incomplete possession activates ownership valuation. Objects held for 30 minutes are valued significantly higher than objects not yet touched.

Boundary conditions: the effect is weaker for goods acquired for resale (traders are less susceptible than non-traders), for goods with clear market prices available, and in collectivist cultural contexts (stronger in individualist cultures).

### Evidence Base

Kahneman, Knetsch & Thaler (1990): participants randomly assigned to receive a coffee mug demanded approximately twice as much to sell it ($7.12 median WTA) as non-owners were willing to pay for it ($3.12 median WTP). This experiment has been replicated extensively.

Hedgcock & Rao (2009): neuroimaging evidence shows that endowment effect involves differential activation of the insula (associated with pain/loss) when contemplating selling versus buying equivalent goods. The neural substrate confirms the loss-aversion account.

Effect size g = 0.57 (Horowitz & McConnell, 2002, meta-analysis across 45 studies). The WTA/WTP ratio averages 2:1 across goods categories, with higher ratios for non-market goods (environmental amenities, health) and lower ratios for consumer goods with established market prices.

Capuchin monkeys demonstrate the endowment effect when given the choice to exchange a received food item for an equally preferred alternative — most refuse the exchange. This suggests the bias has deep evolutionary roots, not solely cultural conditioning.

### Domain Manifestations

**Business.** Companies overvalue their own intellectual property, products, and brand assets relative to market valuation — producing M&A pricing failures in both directions (overpaying for acquisitions because the target company overvalues its assets; undervaluing own assets in divestitures because of endowment effects reversed). Employees overvalue their current roles and compensation packages relative to comparable market alternatives.

**Finance.** Investors overvalue securities they currently hold, creating a disposition effect: profitable positions are sold too early (the gain reduces the ownership attachment), while losing positions are held too long (selling would crystallize the loss). The endowment effect compounds the sunk cost fallacy in portfolio management.

**Personal.** Sellers price possessions above market rates based on personal attachment. Free trial periods exploit the endowment effect deliberately: after 30 days of use, the product feels owned, and cancellation activates loss aversion. Consumers retain gym memberships, subscription services, and possessions past the point of utility because ownership increases subjective value.

### Debiasing Procedure

1. Establish the market reference price for the good before evaluating your own valuation. What would you pay to acquire this good if you did not own it?
2. Apply the opportunity cost frame: what is the best alternative use of the resources this good represents? Ownership should be evaluated against this opportunity cost.
3. Conduct a reversal test: "If I did not own this, would I purchase it today at the price I am demanding to sell it?" If no, the valuation is endowment-inflated.
4. Assign valuation to someone who does not own the good. Use their estimate as a market-clearing price anchor.
5. For organizational assets: use discounted cash flow analysis anchored on prospective value, not acquisition cost or current book value.

### Example

A founder values her company at $15 million in acquisition negotiations, based on what she has built and invested emotionally over six years. Comparable companies with similar metrics have sold at 4–6x revenue, which implies $8–12 million. The gap between her valuation and market comparables is partially endowment effect. Debiasing: ask an independent financial advisor who has no emotional investment in the company to produce a comparable transactions analysis.

## Affect Heuristic

**Tier 2 | Effect size: moderate (varies by domain) | Replication: Robust in risk-benefit studies**

### Mechanism

The affect heuristic is the tendency to use current emotional state and immediate affective responses as informational inputs to judgments about risk, benefit, probability, and quality. When something feels good, people judge it as low-risk and high-benefit; when something feels bad or threatening, they judge it as high-risk and low-benefit, regardless of objective risk-benefit relationships.

The core mechanism: affective tags are attached to mental representations during initial evaluation. When these representations are retrieved for judgment, the affective tag is retrieved simultaneously and serves as an input to the judgment. This shortcut bypasses detailed cognitive evaluation of objective attributes.

The critical empirical signature is the inverse risk-benefit correlation: in domains where objective risk and benefit are uncorrelated (or positively correlated), people perceive them as strongly inversely correlated. Risky activities are perceived as low-benefit; beneficial activities are perceived as low-risk. This inversion is driven by the shared affective valence tag — a single positive/negative feeling drives both the benefit estimate upward and the risk estimate downward (or vice versa).

Slovic (2002) documented that this inverse correlation holds even among domain experts under time pressure. The heuristic becomes dominant when:
- Time pressure is high
- Information load is high
- Emotional salience of the stimulus is high
- Analytical resources are depleted

### Evidence Base

Slovic, Finucane, Peters & MacGregor (2002): participants shown information that increased the perceived benefit of a technology showed reduced risk estimates for the same technology — despite no new risk information. The benefit information propagated through the affective system to update the risk judgment. This asymmetric information propagation is the diagnostic signature.

Finucane et al. (2000): time pressure significantly increases the inverse risk-benefit correlation, confirming that the heuristic dominates when analytical processing is constrained.

Alhakami & Slovic (1994): the inverse correlation between perceived risk and perceived benefit of activities (e.g., nuclear power, pesticides, handguns) was predicted by the strength of positive or negative affect associated with each activity, not by objective risk-benefit data.

Domain-specific effect sizes: large in health and environmental risk perception studies (r ≈ −0.50 to −0.70 for risk-benefit inverse correlation induced by affect manipulation); moderate in consumer product judgments (r ≈ −0.25 to −0.40).

### Domain Manifestations

**Business.** Product teams with high enthusiasm for their product concept systematically underestimate implementation risk and overestimate market benefit — a pattern driven partly by affect heuristic. Evaluations of new technologies or business models are contaminated by the evaluator's general attitude toward novelty or tradition.

**Finance.** Investments in companies associated with positive social affect (e.g., companies with strong ESG narratives, charismatic founders) receive artificially compressed risk assessments relative to fundamentals. Conversely, industries with negative affect associations (tobacco, weapons) may receive inflated risk estimates that create mispricing opportunities.

**Personal.** Health decisions are strongly affect-contaminated: activities that feel healthy (yoga, organic food) are judged as lower risk and higher benefit than objective evidence supports, while activities with negative affective associations are judged as higher risk and lower benefit than evidence warrants. Medical decisions made under strong emotional arousal (fear of diagnosis) systematically distort both risk and benefit estimates.

### Debiasing Procedure

1. Identify the primary affective valence of the stimulus: is the initial gut response strongly positive or strongly negative?
2. Treat affective response as a signal of potential bias, not as evidence. Ask: "What is the objective risk evidence? What is the objective benefit evidence? Are they actually inversely correlated?"
3. Separate risk analysis from benefit analysis. Evaluate each independently before combining. Never simultaneously estimate risk and benefit of a strongly valenced stimulus.
4. Increase deliberation time and reduce time pressure before decisions with high affective loading. The heuristic is most dominant under time constraint.
5. Seek disconfirming information in the underweighted dimension: if something feels good (low risk perception), actively seek risk evidence. If something feels bad (low benefit perception), actively seek benefit evidence.

### Example

A startup founder presents to an investment committee. The founder is highly charismatic and the product concept is exciting. Committee members' affective response to the presentation is strongly positive. In their subsequent analysis, they systematically underestimate execution risk and overestimate addressable market. Debiasing: separate the pitch evaluation from the investment analysis. Have analysts who did not attend the presentation conduct the independent risk analysis.

## In-Group Bias

**Tier 2 | Effect size: d = 0.32 | Replication: Robust across minimal group paradigm studies**

### Mechanism

In-group bias is the tendency to favor members of one's own social group over members of other groups in allocation of resources, evaluation of performance, interpretation of ambiguous behavior, and willingness to cooperate. The bias operates even when group membership is trivially assigned (e.g., random assignment to "Team A" vs. "Team B"), demonstrating that shared identity itself, not any objective difference between groups, drives preferential treatment.

The theoretical foundation is Tajfel and Turner's Social Identity Theory (1979): individuals derive part of their self-concept from group memberships. Because group status reflects on individual self-esteem, members are motivated to view their group positively relative to other groups. This motivation produces in-group favoritism not primarily through hostility toward out-groups, but through the allocation of preferential positive regard to in-group members.

Mechanistic pathways:
- **Categorization**: the moment group membership is salient, evaluation is contaminated by group category membership
- **Self-esteem maintenance**: favorable in-group evaluation is self-serving because group status contributes to individual self-concept
- **Reciprocity expectations**: members expect reciprocal preferential treatment from in-group members, making in-group favor strategically rational in certain contexts (but creating systematic bias when the expectation is unwarranted)
- **Neural encoding**: medial prefrontal cortex activates when processing in-group members — the same region active in self-referential processing — suggesting in-group members are neurally encoded as self-proximate

### Evidence Base

Tajfel et al. (1971) established the Minimal Group Paradigm: participants randomly assigned to groups labeled "Klee fans" or "Kandinsky fans" consistently allocated more points to in-group members in resource allocation tasks, even when strategy favored equal allocation for maximum personal gain. The effect is driven by the desire to maximize the in-group/out-group difference, not absolute in-group gain.

Meta-analysis (Mullen, Brown & Smith, 1992): d = 0.32 across minimal group paradigm studies. Effect size is larger in naturalistic group contexts (d ≈ 0.45–0.65) where group membership is meaningful and identity-relevant.

Neuroimaging (Molenberghs, 2013): viewing in-group members in pain activates neural empathy networks (anterior insula, anterior cingulate cortex) more strongly than viewing out-group members in equivalent pain. This reduced empathic response to out-group suffering is partially automatic and partially modulated by deliberate processing.

Yamagishi experiments: in-group bias in cooperation disappears when mutual monitoring and enforcement mechanisms are removed, suggesting the effect operates partly through expectation of reciprocity rather than pure affiliation.

### Domain Manifestations

**Business.** Hiring decisions favor candidates from the interviewer's university, previous employer, or social background, even when objective qualifications are equivalent. Team leaders allocate high-visibility projects, mentorship, and performance evaluations preferentially to in-group members (same gender, ethnicity, nationality, educational background). Procurement decisions favor vendors with in-group connections over objectively superior alternatives.

**Finance.** Home bias in investment: investors overweight domestic equities relative to optimal portfolio theory, driven by affiliation with familiar entities. Analysts issue more favorable ratings for firms geographically or culturally proximate to their own location. Venture investors fund founders who share their background at rates inconsistent with deal quality.

**Personal.** Information from in-group sources is weighted more heavily and subjected to less critical scrutiny than identical information from out-group sources. Ambiguous behavior by in-group members is interpreted charitably; identical behavior by out-group members is interpreted negatively.

### Debiasing Procedure

1. Identify active in-group/out-group categorization. Is group membership salient in this evaluation context? What group is serving as the in-group?
2. Blind the evaluator to group membership where possible. In hiring, remove university names, zip codes, and other identity-correlating attributes from initial screening.
3. Use structured evaluation criteria established before evaluating any candidate or option. Pre-commitment to criteria reduces post-hoc rationalization driven by in-group preference.
4. Create superordinate group identity: reframe the context so that the relevant in-group expands to include the current out-group. Robbers Cave experiments (Sherif, 1961) demonstrated that introducing shared goals reduced inter-group hostility and bias substantially.
5. Require explicit justification for resource allocation decisions. The accountability requirement activates analytical processing and reduces automatic in-group favoritism by approximately 0.10–0.15d.

### Example

A venture capital partnership evaluates two comparable startups for investment. Founder A attended the same MBA program as two partners; Founder B has a non-traditional background. The partnership allocates disproportionate time to due diligence on Founder A's company and interprets ambiguous financial projections favorably. Founder B's equally ambiguous projections receive more scrutiny. Debiasing: establish a standardized due diligence checklist applied identically to all companies at the same stage, completed by analysts without prior relationship to either founder.

## Cross-Bias Interactions in Decision-Framing Contexts

These six biases frequently co-occur and amplify each other:

**Framing + Sunk Cost**: sunk costs are more likely to influence behavior when framed as losses to be recovered rather than as assets already spent. The loss frame activates the sunk cost mechanism more powerfully.

**Sunk Cost + Commitment Bias**: the sunk cost provides the emotional content (I have invested so much), while commitment bias provides the social-identity justification (changing course means admitting error). Together they produce escalation that neither would generate alone.

**Endowment Effect + Framing**: selling an owned asset activates loss framing automatically because ownership defines the reference point. The endowment effect partly operates through the framing effect applied to a specific reference point.

**Affect Heuristic + In-Group Bias**: in-group members generate positive affect, which contaminates risk and benefit assessments through the affect heuristic. In-group favoritism in evaluation is amplified by the affect heuristic producing artificially favorable risk-benefit assessments for in-group work.

**Commitment Bias + In-Group Bias**: public commitments made within an in-group context are especially resistant to revision because changing position threatens both personal consistency and in-group standing simultaneously.

When multiple biases are co-active, debiasing any single one is insufficient. The procedural interventions are most effective when applied structurally (pre-commitment criteria, blind evaluation, structured justification requirements) rather than attempted cognitively in the moment.
