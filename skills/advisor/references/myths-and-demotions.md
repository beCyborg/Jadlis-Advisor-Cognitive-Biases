# Myths and Demotions: Biases That Evidence Has Challenged or Overturned

This file exists for one purpose: to prevent Claude from confidently citing debunked or severely overstated biases as if they were established facts. These are the most cited, most popularly referenced biases in popular psychology — and they are either statistical artifacts, failed replications, or wildly inflated beyond what evidence supports.

When a user mentions any of these by name, do not simply validate the popular claim. Apply the correction protocol for each one.

---

## Loss Aversion — DEMOTED

**Popular tier: foundational, universally cited → Evidence tier: Tier 4, Rank 61**

### The Myth

Losses hurt psychologically approximately twice as much as equivalent gains feel good — the lambda (loss aversion coefficient) is approximately 2.0, as established by Kahneman and Tversky's Prospect Theory (1979, 1992). This ratio is treated as a near-universal constant of human psychology.

### The Evidence

The 2x ratio is not supported by modern meta-analytic evidence:

- **Walasek et al. (2024)** conducted a large-scale reanalysis and found lambda = **1.31**, not 2.0. The classic estimate was inflated by methodological choices in how choices were elicited and analyzed.
- **Yechiam (2025)** found lambda = **1.07** — barely above 1.0, meaning nearly no loss aversion — when controlling for response asymmetry and ordering effects in choice tasks. At this level, the effect is substantively trivial.
- **Gal & Rucker (2018)** argued in a major review that loss aversion is a label applied to phenomena better explained by other mechanisms (status quo bias, inertia, risk aversion proper).
- Individual differences are enormous: some people show gain-seeking under loss framing. Loss aversion is weaker or absent in populations with high power, high wealth, collectivist cultural contexts (Wang's 53-country study found Eastern European countries highest, African countries lowest).

### What's Actually Happening

There is a real asymmetry between gains and losses in some contexts — but it is smaller, more variable, and more context-dependent than Prospect Theory's canonical lambda suggests. What looks like "loss aversion" is often:
- **Status quo bias**: preference for the current state, not specifically fear of loss
- **Risk aversion**: preference for certainty over variance, which operates independently of gain/loss framing
- **Attention asymmetry**: losses command more attention, but this doesn't translate to 2x motivational weight in real decisions

The foundational Prospect Theory framework remains valuable. The specific 2x constant is not reliable.

### Correction Protocol

When a user invokes loss aversion:
1. Acknowledge: "Loss aversion is a real concept from Prospect Theory — people do show asymmetric responses to gains and losses in many contexts."
2. Correct the magnitude: "The popular '2x' ratio is not supported. Meta-analyses find lambda between 1.07 and 1.31, not 2.0. The effect exists but is much smaller and more variable than commonly claimed."
3. Redirect: Ask whether what they're describing is better explained by status quo bias (reluctance to change current state regardless of gain/loss framing), risk aversion (preference for certainty), or regret aversion (avoiding decisions that could be blamed later).
4. Practical implication: The advice to "frame things as losses to double their motivational impact" is not reliably supported. Loss framing increases attention, not necessarily a 2x motivational effect.

## Dunning-Kruger Effect — DEMOTED

**Popular tier: widely cited metacognitive law → Evidence tier: Tier 4, Rank 62**

### The Myth

Unskilled people dramatically overestimate their competence because they lack the metacognitive ability to recognize their own incompetence. The original Dunning & Kruger (1999) data showed bottom-quartile performers estimating themselves at the 62nd percentile. This has been canonized as a fundamental truth about human self-assessment.

### The Evidence

The pattern in the original data is a **statistical artifact**, not a metacognitive phenomenon:

- **Gignac & Zajenkowski (2020)** demonstrated that the Dunning-Kruger pattern is reproduced when you substitute **random data** for actual performance scores. The characteristic curve — where low performers overestimate and high performers underestimate — emerges purely from **autocorrelation** between the predictor (actual ability) and the criterion (estimated ability minus actual ability). When actual ability is subtracted from estimated ability to create "overestimation," you mathematically guarantee that lower performers appear to overestimate and higher performers appear to underestimate.
- **Nuhfer et al. (2016, 2017)** analyzed large datasets using signal-to-noise ratio methods and found that self-assessment accuracy improves monotonically with skill level — no "peak of Mount Stupid," no valley of humility in the pattern. Self-assessment is simply noisy at all skill levels.
- **Krajc & Ortmann (2008)** showed the effect disappears or inverts under certain incentive structures and task types.
- The specific claim that low performers overestimate most dramatically is driven by regression to the mean: if you score at the bottom of any scale and estimate your rank, you will regress toward the middle by mathematical necessity.

### What's Actually Happening

The real, replicable phenomenon is simpler and less dramatic: **people are moderately inaccurate at self-assessment, and accuracy improves with domain knowledge**. Novices do not necessarily overestimate dramatically — they are simply uncertain and their estimates are noisy. Experts are more calibrated because they have more reference points.

Additionally, a separate real phenomenon is **overconfidence bias** in specific domains (people overestimate their performance on hard tasks, underestimate on easy tasks — the hard-easy effect). This is robust and not an artifact. But it's not specifically about unskilled people being unaware of their incompetence.

### Correction Protocol

When a user invokes Dunning-Kruger:
1. Acknowledge: "The Dunning-Kruger effect is extremely widely cited — the claim that unskilled people dramatically overestimate their competence."
2. Correct: "The original pattern has been shown to be a statistical artifact. Gignac & Zajenkowski (2020) reproduced the same curve using random data, demonstrating it follows mathematically from how overestimation is calculated, not from any psychological mechanism."
3. Redirect to the real phenomenon: If their situation involves someone who seems unaware of their incompetence, more accurate framings are: **overconfidence bias** (robust, domain-specific), **lack of feedback loops** in their environment, or **motivated reasoning** (not wanting to see their errors). These have stronger empirical support.
4. Practical implication: Don't assume that someone's confident ignorance follows a predictable arc toward humility as they learn. Calibration is individual, domain-specific, and feedback-dependent.

## Choice Overload — DEMOTED

**Popular tier: established consumer behavior law → Evidence tier: Tier 5, Rank 92**

### The Myth

More options paralyze decision-making. The famous Iyengar & Lepper (2000) "jam study" showed 24 options produced 3% purchase rate vs. 30% for 6 options — a 10x difference. More options lead to decision avoidance, reduced satisfaction, and worse choices. Barry Schwartz's "Paradox of Choice" built an entire framework on this.

### The Evidence

The jam study result does not replicate, and meta-analysis finds effectively no effect:

- **Scheibehenne, Greifeneder & Todd (2010)** conducted a meta-analysis of 50 experiments on choice overload: mean effect size **d = 0.02** — statistically and practically indistinguishable from zero. Some studies showed overload, others showed that more choice increased engagement and purchase. The effect is not consistent or reliable.
- The original jam study has **never been successfully replicated** with the same methodology. Iyengar's lab has not published a replication.
- **Chernev, Böckenholt & Goodman (2015)** meta-analysis identified four moderating conditions under which overload does appear: high choice-set complexity, low assortability, preference uncertainty, and decision difficulty. Without these conditions, more choice does not harm decisions.
- The effect, when it appears, depends heavily on whether the options are clearly differentiated and whether the chooser has prior preferences.

### What's Actually Happening

Choice overload is real under specific conditions: when options are complex, hard to compare, the chooser has no prior preferences, and there is no dominant default. These conditions must all be present. For routine decisions with familiar categories, more options typically do not cause paralysis.

The more robust related phenomena are: **satisficing vs. maximizing strategies** (maximizers suffer more from large option sets, satisficers don't) and **opportunity cost salience** (being reminded of what you're giving up when choosing increases regret, independently of option count).

### Correction Protocol

When a user invokes choice overload:
1. Acknowledge: "The idea that more options cause decision paralysis is very intuitive and widely cited."
2. Correct: "The meta-analytic evidence doesn't support this as a general law. Scheibehenne et al. (2010) analyzed 50 studies and found d = 0.02 — essentially zero. The original jam study never replicated."
3. Assess conditions: Ask whether the options are genuinely hard to compare (complex, incommensurable), whether the person has clear preferences, and whether there is a clear default. If yes to complexity and no to preferences, choice overload conditions may be present.
4. Redirect to more robust mechanisms: If someone is avoiding a decision, **ambiguity aversion**, **status quo bias**, or **anticipated regret** are better-supported explanations than raw option count.

## Ego Depletion / Decision Fatigue — DEMOTED

**Popular tier: foundational willpower science → Evidence tier: Tier 5, Rank 91**

### The Myth

Willpower is a finite cognitive resource that depletes with use, like a muscle that tires. Making decisions, resisting temptations, or exerting self-control draws from this limited pool. Once depleted, decision quality collapses — the "ego depletion" model, foundational to Baumeister's self-control theory. Widely popularized through the Israeli parole study (judges grant fewer paroles as the day progresses) and glucose depletion narratives.

### The Evidence

The core ego depletion effect has not replicated:

- **Hagger et al. (2016)** ran a pre-registered **23-laboratory Registered Replication Report (RRR)** — the most rigorous replication attempt — with N = 2,141. Effect size: **d = 0.04** (95% CI: -0.07 to 0.15). Not statistically significant. The original d = 0.62 from meta-analyses was almost certainly inflated by publication bias.
- **Carter et al. (2015)** reanalyzed the ego depletion meta-analytic dataset and found strong evidence of publication bias using the Egger test and p-curve analysis. Correcting for this reduces the effect close to zero.
- The **Israeli parole study** (Danziger et al., 2011) — judges granting fewer paroles before breaks — has been reanalyzed. Keren & Schul (2009) and subsequent work identified **alternative explanations**: the case ordering was not random, easier/more favorable cases clustered in the early morning session, and the dip before breaks coincides with harder cases, not cognitive fatigue.
- The glucose mechanism is not supported: providing glucose does not reliably restore "depleted" willpower in controlled studies.

### What's Actually Happening

There are real phenomena in the neighborhood: people do make worse decisions under sleep deprivation, under high cognitive load, and under time pressure. These are robust. However, the specific mechanism — that willpower is a depletable resource that governs self-control across all domains — is not supported.

What may be real: **motivation** and **perceived difficulty** fluctuate across a day. People conserve effort strategically. The "depletion" framing may be a self-fulfilling prophecy — believing willpower is finite makes it behave as if it is (**Inzlicht & Schmeichel, 2012**, motivational model).

### Correction Protocol

When a user invokes ego depletion or decision fatigue:
1. Acknowledge: "The idea that willpower is a finite resource, popularized by Baumeister, has been enormously influential."
2. Correct with specifics: "The Registered Replication Report by Hagger et al. (2016), with 23 labs and 2,141 participants, found d = 0.04 — a null result. The original effects appear to have been inflated by publication bias."
3. Distinguish what is real: **Cognitive load** genuinely impairs decision quality in the moment. **Sleep deprivation** has robust effects. **Motivation depletion** is a better model than resource depletion.
4. Practical implication: Advice to "make important decisions in the morning before your willpower depletes" is not well-supported. Advice to reduce cognitive load, sleep adequately, and not make high-stakes decisions under time pressure remains valid for different reasons.

## Hot-Hand "Fallacy" — INVERTED (the fallacy was itself fallacious)

**Popular tier: classic cognitive bias, textbook example → Evidence tier: Tier 5, Rank 95**

### The Myth

The belief that a basketball player on a streak is "hot" and more likely to make the next shot is a cognitive fallacy — people incorrectly perceive patterns in random sequences (the gambler's fallacy applied to skilled performance). Gilovich, Vallone & Tversky (1985) claimed to show no autocorrelation in shooting data. The hot hand was declared an illusion.

### The Evidence

This is uniquely inverted: **the "fallacy" was itself the error**. The original study was statistically flawed, and the hot hand effect is real.

- **Miller & Sanjurjo (2018)**, published in *Econometrica*, identified a fundamental statistical bias in the Gilovich et al. (1985) methodology. When you calculate the probability of a hit following a streak of hits from a **finite sequence of coin flips** (or shots), you introduce a **selection bias**: hits following streaks are systematically underrepresented in small samples. The original analysis was biased against finding autocorrelation even if it existed.
- After correcting for this bias, Miller & Sanjurjo reanalyzed the original GVT data and found **statistically significant positive autocorrelation** — the hot hand effect was present in the original data all along.
- Subsequent analyses of NBA three-point contests, baseball, and bowling find genuine hot hand effects after correcting for the bias.
- The effect is domain-specific: it applies to **skilled human performance** (where physiological state, confidence, and momentum plausibly affect outcomes) but not to purely probabilistic mechanisms like roulette wheels or dice.

### What's Actually Happening

Skilled performance in sports does exhibit genuine streakiness beyond what chance predicts, likely through real mechanisms: kinesthetic calibration (a shooter who has found their arc maintains it), confidence effects on attention and execution, and fatigue operating in reverse (a player in rhythm is not fighting mechanics). This is qualitatively different from the gambler's fallacy, which involves belief in streaks in genuinely random processes.

The gambler's fallacy (expecting a coin to "correct" after a run of heads) remains a real, robust error. The hot-hand fallacy, as a description of irrational belief in skilled performance streaks, is not.

### Correction Protocol

When a user invokes the hot-hand fallacy as an example of cognitive bias:
1. Flag the inversion immediately: "The hot-hand fallacy is a special case — the original research claiming it was a fallacy was itself statistically flawed."
2. Explain the correction: "Miller & Sanjurjo (2018) in *Econometrica* identified a selection bias in the original 1985 methodology. When corrected, the original data actually shows significant positive autocorrelation — the hot hand effect was real and the study failed to detect it."
3. Draw the correct distinction: The **gambler's fallacy** (expecting random sequences to self-correct) is a robust, well-evidenced error. The **hot hand effect** in skilled human performance appears to be real. Conflating them under "streak fallacy" is incorrect.
4. Practical implication: When evaluating human skilled performance streaks (sales records, creative output, athletic performance), treating all streaks as random noise is unjustified. There may be real signal. Statistical analysis requiring large samples and corrected methodology is needed before concluding either way.

## Social / Behavioral Priming — DEMOTED

**Popular tier: powerful behavioral science → Evidence tier: Tier 5, Rank 93**

### The Myth

Subtle, subliminal environmental cues powerfully influence complex behavior. Exposure to words associated with the elderly makes people walk slower (Bargh et al., 1996). Exposure to money primes self-sufficiency. Holding warm coffee makes you perceive others as warmer. Small environmental manipulations produce large, reliable behavioral effects. This was a central pillar of popularized behavioral science, including Kahneman's *Thinking, Fast and Slow*.

### The Evidence

The behavioral priming literature has failed replication systematically:

- **Many Labs Replication Project** tested multiple priming effects across dozens of labs. Behavioral priming effects replicated at approximately **36%** — meaning roughly two-thirds failed. Crucially, the most dramatic and counterintuitive effects (the ones that built the field's reputation) replicated least well.
- **Bargh's "elderly priming" study** (the most famous — reading words like "Florida," "bingo," "wrinkle" causes slower walking) was directly replicated by Doyen et al. (2012) using blinded measurement of walking speed. **Effect was absent** when experimenters didn't know the hypothesis. The original effect appears to have been an experimenter expectancy artifact.
- **Daniel Kahneman**, who popularized priming in *Thinking, Fast and Slow*, wrote an open letter to priming researchers in 2012 urging the field to shore up its evidence base, essentially acknowledging that the literature was fragile. He has since publicly acknowledged that his treatment of social priming overstated the evidence.
- The **"ego depletion via glucose" priming** (drinking lemonade restores willpower), **"heavy clipboards make things seem more important"** studies, and numerous others in this genre failed replication.

### What's Actually Happening

**Semantic priming** — where exposure to a concept speeds recognition of related concepts (e.g., "doctor" primes faster recognition of "nurse") — is robust and replicates reliably. This is a property of how associative memory works. It is a narrow, cognitive/perceptual effect.

**Behavioral priming** — where semantic activation translates into measurable changes in complex behavior (walking speed, helping behavior, aggression) — is largely not replicable at the effect sizes originally reported. The distinction matters: priming thoughts is real; priming behavior through subtle cues is not reliably real at meaningful magnitudes.

Priming effects that do replicate tend to be: close in time to measurement, using explicit cues rather than subliminal ones, and measuring reaction times rather than naturalistic behavior.

### Correction Protocol

When a user invokes social priming (environmental cues changing behavior):
1. Distinguish: "Priming as a cognitive phenomenon — activating related concepts in memory — is real and well-supported. Behavioral priming — where subtle environmental cues produce large changes in actual behavior — is not."
2. Cite the replication record: "The Many Labs project found ~36% replication for behavioral priming effects. Bargh's elderly walking study failed to replicate when walking speed was measured blind to condition. Kahneman publicly acknowledged the priming literature was overstated."
3. Redirect to what is robust: If the user is interested in environmental influences on behavior, **implementation intentions** (explicit if-then planning), **defaults and choice architecture** (opt-in vs. opt-out), and **social norms framing** have far better replication records than behavioral priming.
4. Practical implication: Do not design interventions around subtle semantic primes (using "winning" language in emails, rearranging office furniture to prime achievement). These effects are either absent or too small to matter in practice. Use explicit framing, defaults, and social proof instead.

## Quick Reference: When to Apply Corrections

| User says... | Correct response |
|---|---|
| "Losses hurt twice as much as gains" | Lambda is ~1.07–1.31, not 2.0 (Walasek 2024, Yechiam 2025) |
| "Dunning-Kruger means unskilled people don't know they're bad" | Pattern is a statistical artifact from autocorrelation (Gignac 2020) |
| "More choices cause decision paralysis" | Meta-analysis finds d = 0.02 (Scheibehenne 2010); effect is near zero |
| "Willpower depletes like a muscle" | 23-lab RRR found d = 0.04 (Hagger 2016); ego depletion did not replicate |
| "The hot hand is a fallacy" | Original study was statistically flawed; Miller & Sanjurjo 2018 found real effect |
| "Subtle primes powerfully change behavior" | Behavioral priming ~36% replication; Bargh's walking study failed blind replication |

Apply these corrections with specific citations and effect sizes. Vague hedging ("some researchers question this") understates the problem. These are not minor controversies — they are systematic replication failures and methodological critiques published in top journals.
